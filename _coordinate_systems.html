Coordinate Systems

Transformation in Detail

Volume Space

The volume data is uploaded to video memory as a 3D texture. Texture coordinates are conventionally specified as \( [ 0, 1 ]^n\). For \( n = 3 \), this is what Carna refers to as volume space or texture space coordinates, it's vector components being the vertex' coordinates on the x-, y-, and z-axis of the 3D texture:

\[ v_\text{volumespace} = \begin{pmatrix}u\\s\\t\end{pmatrix} \quad \text{with} \quad u, s, t \in [0,1] \]

Model Space

The model space or object space is based on a scaling of the volume space. It is defined as

\[ v_\text{modelspace} = \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} (\text{width} - 1) \cdot \Delta x & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & (\text{height} - 1) \cdot \Delta y & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & (\text{depth} - 1) \cdot \Delta z & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}}_{M_0} \cdot v_\text{volumespace} \]

with \(\text{width}\), \(\text{height}\), \(\text{depth}\) being the number of voxels along the texture's x-, y- and z-axis; and \(\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta z\) being the spacings between two neighboring voxels along the particular axis in millimeters. Therefore, model units are also millimeters.

The transformation between those two spaces is encapsulated by the Position class:

  • Position::toMillimetersTransformation computes the \(M_0\) matrix.
  • Position::toVolumeUnitsTransformation computes the \(M_0^{-1}\) matrix.

Scene Space

The scene matrix \(S\) maps from volume space into scene space:

\[ S = S_0 \cdot M_0 \]

At simplest - and this is what applies for the default implementation - the scene space equals the model space, implying \( S_0 \) being identity. However, any particular implementation is free to use any different \( S_0 \) with

\[ S_0 = \begin{pmatrix} r_{11} & r_{12} & r_{13} & t_x \\ r_{21} & r_{22} & r_{23} & t_y \\ r_{31} & r_{32} & r_{33} & t_z \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \text{ and } \sqrt{r_{1j}^2 + r_{2j}^2 + r_{3j}^2} = 1 \text{ for } j = 1, 2, 3\]

Hence, scene units - the units used to specify scene space coordinates - are millimeters too.

Camera Space

The camera space is also commonly refered to as view space and eye space. The camera implementation is free to use any camera matrix \(C\). Relatively to the scene space, the camera space is defined as follows:

\[ v_\text{cameraspace} = C \cdot v_\text{scenespace} \]

Screen Space

Note:
The screen space in Carna corresponds to what is called clipping coordinate system in computer graphics traditionally. The different term in Carna results from historical reasons.

The camera implementation is free to use any projection matrix \(P\). The transformation is defined as:

\[ v_\text{screenspace} = P \cdot v_\text{cameraspace} \]

The full transformation of any drawn vertex is defined as:

\[ v_\text{screenspace} = P \cdot C \cdot S \cdot v_\text{volumespace} = P \cdot C \cdot S_0 \cdot M_0 \cdot v_\text{volumespace} \]

In screenspace, each vertex \(v_\text{screenspace}=\left( x_\text{C}, y_\text{C}, z_\text{C}, w_\text{C} \right)\) is within the view frustum if and only if:

\[ -w_\text{C} \leq x_\text{C} \leq w_\text{C} \wedge -w_\text{C} \leq y_\text{C} \leq w_\text{C} \wedge -w_\text{C} \leq z_\text{C} \leq w_\text{C} \]

Fixed OpenGL Transformations

The screenspace coordinates are then transformed into normalized device coordinates.

This morphs the view frustum into a cuboid shape:

\[ v_\text{nd} = \left( x_\text{nd}, y_\text{nd}, z_\text{nd} \right) = \left( \frac{x_\text{C}}{w_\text{C}}, \frac{y_\text{C}}{w_\text{C}}, \frac{z_\text{C}}{w_\text{C}} \right) \]

Here, the visible space is identified by \([-1, +1]^2 \times [0, 1]\). These coordinates are scaled to the window coordinates \( v_\text{wnd} = \left( x_\text{wnd}, y_\text{wnd}, z_\text{wnd} \right) \subset \mathbb{N}^2 \times [0, 1] \) as a final step, corresponding to following rules:

  • \(z_\text{wnd} = z_\text{nd}\)
  • \(\left( -1, y_\text{nd}, z_\text{nd} \right)\) is mapped to the left-most pixel column \(\left( 0, y_\text{wnd}, z_\text{wnd} \right)\).
  • \(\left( x_\text{nd}, -1, z_\text{nd} \right)\) is mapped to the top-most pixel row \(\left( x_\text{wnd}, 0, z_\text{wnd} \right)\).
